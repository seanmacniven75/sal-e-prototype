About this research

Thank you for participating in SAL-E.

This study is part of research at King's College London on how people pay attention to faces. We're interested in understanding how attention works in different people, and how we can measure it using everyday smartphones rather than specialised laboratory equipment.

Over the next two weeks you'll complete five short sessions like this one. Each session takes about 10 minutes and involves looking at photographs of faces. You don't need to do anything except look at the screen naturally.

The information we collect will help us understand attention patterns in social anxiety and may eventually help develop better assessment tools for mental health support.

Your data is encrypted, anonymised, and stored securely. You can withdraw at any time without giving a reason.

How the session works

        

            
What you'll see

            
You'll see two faces on screen at a time. The faces show different emotions including happy, sad, angry, and neutral expressions. Each pair will be shown for a few seconds.

        

        

            
What to do

            
Please look at the screen naturally. There is no correct way to look at the faces. You don't need to identify the emotions, react to them, or do anything specific. Just look at the screen as you normally would when looking at people. Blinking is completely fine.

        

        

            
What to know

            
Try to keep the phone at a comfortable reading distance. Hold it steady. The session has 3 short blocks with breaks between them. You can stop at any time using STOP SESSION at the top of the screen.

        

        

            
        

    


    
    

        
Quick calibration

        
We need to calibrate the camera to your eyes. Please look at the dot wherever it appears on the screen and follow it with your gaze. This takes about 30 seconds.

        
On this web prototype, camera depth and ARKit capture are simulated; a native build will record TrueDepth distance, gaze, and eye transforms per point.
